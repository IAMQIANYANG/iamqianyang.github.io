Why visualization?

In the field of data visualization, it is believed that visual techniques used to represent and present data are able to make full use of the human eyesâ€™ broad bandwidth pathway, and make it possible for people to see and to interpret large amounts of information at once (Thomas & Cook, 2005).

The image at the left is an example of data visualization in the form of map (click on to view the original graph), it indicates the number of reported corruptions in the world in 2013. In this map, yellow indicates a lower level of corruption, while rose indicates a higher level, (white areas stand for not recorded).

From this map, we could immediately see that which countries have the highest corruptions reported.

Imagine, if the table is used to represent these data, how would it look like? One thing for sure is that it would definitely take longer time for the audience to understand the information.

One of the most compelling arguments for the value of data visualization is expressed in this quote from John.W.Tukey (1997):

The greatest value of a picture is when it forces us to notice what we never expected to see.

Through visualization, we are seeking to portray data in ways that allow us to see it in a new light, to visually observe patterns, exceptions, and the possible stories that sit behind its raw state. This is about considering visualization as a tool for discovery(Kirk, 2012).
